Cognitive Speed Training

Evidence-based brain training exercises shown to reduce dementia risk. Based on the ACTIVE study and UFOV research.

The Science Behind Speed Training

The ACTIVE Study

The Advanced Cognitive Training for Independent and Vital Elderly (ACTIVE) study is one of the largest and longest randomised controlled trials of cognitive training ever conducted. Beginning in 1998, it enrolled 2,832 healthy older adults (average age 74) across six sites in the United States.

Participants were randomly assigned to one of four groups: memory training, reasoning training, speed-of-processing training, or a no-contact control group. Each training group received 10 initial sessions of approximately 60 to 75 minutes each, with booster sessions offered at 11 and 35 months.

The Speed Training Advantage

At the 10-year follow-up (Rebok et al., 2014), a remarkable pattern emerged. While all three training groups showed lasting improvements in their trained cognitive abilities, only the speed-of-processing group showed a reduced risk of dementia. Participants who completed speed training had a 29% lower risk of dementia compared to the control group (Edwards et al., 2017).

This effect was dose-dependent: participants who completed more training sessions showed greater protection. Neither the memory training group nor the reasoning training group showed statistically significant reductions in dementia risk.

What Is Speed-of-Processing Training?

Speed-of-processing training is built on the Useful Field of View (UFOV) paradigm developed by Karlene Ball and colleagues. The UFOV measures how quickly and accurately a person can process visual information across their field of view. In training, participants practise identifying and localising briefly presented visual targets, with display durations that adapt to maintain an optimal challenge level.

As training progresses, presentation times shorten from hundreds of milliseconds to as little as 20 to 30 milliseconds. This forces the brain's visual processing systems to work faster and more efficiently, expanding the useful field of view and improving processing speed.

Why Does It Work?

Speed-of-processing training targets the frontoparietal attention network, which governs how quickly and efficiently we process, filter, and respond to visual information. This network shows early vulnerability in preclinical dementia. By strengthening these neural circuits, speed training may build cognitive reserve, helping the brain maintain function even as age-related changes accumulate.

The training also produces transfer effects to everyday activities. ACTIVE study participants who received speed training showed significantly fewer difficulties with instrumental activities of daily living, such as managing finances, using the telephone, and preparing meals (Willis et al., 2006).
